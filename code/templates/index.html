Old Bailey Online Profile

OThis is a comprehensive digital archive of over 197,752 criminal trial records held at London's Central Criminal Court between 1674 and 1913, providing insights into crime, justice and society. This site provides a rich resource for searching for patterns in crime types, demographics, legal outcomes, and more.

Visit official website: Old Bailey Online

Project Introduction

This project, "Old Bailey Online and Visual Analytics - Variant A1: Exploring Crime Types & Trends (1674-1913)", aims to systematically study the criminal trial records on the website of the Central Criminal Court in London (Old Bailey) from 1674 to 1913 by using data science and visual analysis techniques. Through the analysis and visualization of historical crime data, the project not only hopes to reveal the evolution trend of different types of crimes in the past 240 years, but also attempts to explore the social, legal, economic and technological changes reflected behind them, and finally build an interactive web visual analysis platform to provide new historical data to the public, researchers and policy makers.

Research Questions

1. How did the frequency of different crime types (theft, murder, fraud, etc.) change from 1674 to 1913?
2. What was the trend in the proportion of violent crimes (e.g., assault, murder) vs. non-violent crimes (e.g., theft, fraud)?
3. How did the occurrence of capital crimes change across the centuries?
4. Which crime categories had the highest conviction rates?
5. How did the rate of property crimes (e.g., theft, burglary) vary during periods of economic hardship?
6. What were the most frequent crimes committed by individuals under 18?
7. What was the impact of industrialization on crime rates in London?
8. How did the ratio of "crimes of necessity" (e.g., theft, food stealing) change during times of famine or high unemployment?
9. How did technology (e.g., the advent of railways) influence crime patterns?
10. What was the relationship between alcohol-related crimes (e.g., public drunkenness, assault) and public policies like the Gin Act?
